Langauges in Society

By: Allison Patten

Billions of people around the world are trying to learn English as an economic advantage not only in their own country, but globally too. English has been dubbed the official language of business, mathematics, science, aviation, and other fields, according to the Harvard Business Review. When there is a language barrier between employees, or between one branch of a company with another, mistakes happen and jobs are lost. That is why a universal language for a particular fields of work have been established, and a large percentage of them have been established as English.

While there is simply a convenience factor with having the entire workforce speaking the same language, sometimes it can be much more critical. Take aviation for example. Miscommunication between employees could be the difference between life and death.

The countries that speak English collectively have a higher Gross Domestic Product (GDP) than countries that speak any other language, according to Education First . This trend mostly follows suit with individual countries GDP too. This trend is called "Enlishnization".

English holds the highest GDP due to the number of work fields in which it holds the official language. Chinese is next due to it's up-and-coming industrial economy and the sheer population size of China. Japanese follows because of Japan's booming tech industry. Spanish is the fourth largest GDP by language due to the high amount of countries with Spanish as the native tounge.

Since the launch of the term "Englishnization" in 2001, very few career fields have shown consistent strength in English skills. Engineering was the top field but it still has room for improvement. As you look through the data, it is obvious that the fields with a higher English Proficiency Index (EPI) are the more creative core jobs that tend to hire highly educated employees. It makes sense that the simplistic fields lag behind because they do not necessarily require a high level of training.
